CASE: Indra Sarma v. V.K.V. Sarma
YEAR: 2013
COURT: Supreme Court of India
CITATION: (2013) 15 SCC 755
CATEGORY: Domestic Violence / Protection of Women
RULING: The Supreme Court held that a woman in a live-in relationship that resembles marriage is entitled to protection under the Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act 2005. The 'relationship in the nature of marriage' includes long-term live-in relationships and the woman can claim residence rights, maintenance, and protection orders.
KEY PRINCIPLE: Live-in relationships of a matrimonial nature fall within the Domestic Violence Act. Women can claim protection orders, residence orders, and monetary relief.

CASE: Aruna Parmod Shah v. Union of India
YEAR: 2008
COURT: Delhi High Court
CITATION: WP(C) No. 7183/2007
CATEGORY: Domestic Violence / Residence Rights
RULING: The Delhi High Court upheld the constitutional validity of the Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act 2005 and held that a woman has the right to reside in the shared household regardless of whether she has ownership rights. The husband cannot forcibly evict her without obtaining a court order under the Act.
KEY PRINCIPLE: Women have an inalienable right to reside in the matrimonial home. Forcible eviction is an act of domestic violence.

CASE: Shreya Singhal v. Union of India
YEAR: 2015
COURT: Supreme Court of India
CITATION: AIR 2015 SC 1523
CATEGORY: Cyber / IT Act / Free Speech
RULING: The Supreme Court struck down Section 66A of the IT Act 2000 as unconstitutional for being vague and overbroad, violating Article 19(1)(a) (freedom of speech). However, Sections 69A (blocking of websites) and 79 (intermediary liability) were upheld subject to procedural safeguards.
KEY PRINCIPLE: Section 66A of the IT Act is void. Online speech can only be restricted under valid reasonable restrictions in Article 19(2). Victims of online fraud should use Section 66D (cheating by personation using computer) instead.

CASE: State of Tamil Nadu v. Suhas Katti
YEAR: 2004
COURT: Additional Chief Metropolitan Magistrate, Chennai
CITATION: Case No. 4680/2004
CATEGORY: Cyber / Harassment / IT Act
RULING: First conviction under the IT Act 2000. The accused was convicted under Section 67 (publishing obscene material electronically) and Sections 469 and 509 of the IPC for sending obscene messages and posting the victim's personal information online to harass her. Sentenced to rigorous imprisonment.
KEY PRINCIPLE: Online harassment including posting private information and obscene content is punishable under Section 67 IT Act and Section 509 IPC. Cybercrime police can investigate and arrest.

CASE: Infosys BPO Ltd v. Shamsher Martinez
YEAR: 2009
COURT: Madras High Court
CITATION: CS No. 189/2009
CATEGORY: IP / Trade Secret / IT Act
RULING: The Madras High Court granted an injunction restraining a former employee from using or disclosing confidential client data downloaded from the company's systems before resignation. Unauthorized data download from an employer's system constitutes an offence under Section 43 and Section 66 of the IT Act 2000.
KEY PRINCIPLE: Unauthorized access to and download of employer data is an IT Act offence. Injunctions available to protect trade secrets and confidential information.

CASE: Time Incorporated v. Lokesh Srivastava
YEAR: 2005
COURT: Delhi High Court
CITATION: CS(OS) No. 2172/2004
CATEGORY: IP / Copyright / Trademark
RULING: The Delhi High Court held that passing off and trademark infringement are distinct torts. The defendant used a mark similar to TIME magazine's mark for a publication. Punitive damages of Rs 5 lakh were awarded as deterrence even though actual damages were difficult to prove, establishing the principle of punitive damages in IP cases in India.
KEY PRINCIPLE: Punitive damages are available in Indian trademark and copyright cases. Similarity causing confusion in the marketplace is actionable even without proof of actual loss.
